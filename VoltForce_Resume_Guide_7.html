Resume Guide

The Modern Resume:

Built for the Age of AI & ATS

The rules have changed. With applicant tracking systems and AI tools now embedded in nearly every hiring process, the old playbook for resume writing is largely obsolete. This guide cuts through the noise: no generic advice, no outdated templates, just clear, current strategies to help your resume get seen, get read, and get you in the door.

Pass ATS filters consistently Format and optimize your resume so it reaches a human every time.
Tell a story that earns interviews Move from listing duties to proving impact through measurable results.
Know exactly how long your resume should be Stop cutting your experience short to fit outdated conventions.
Stand out in a crowded, AI-screened market Apply strategies built for how hiring actually works today.
01

Formatting Principles

Before a human ever reads your resume, software parses it. Get the structure wrong and you're screened out before you've had a chance to compete. These principles ensure your resume survives both the machine and the manager.

1.1 Use a Simple, Clean Layout
A single-column format with clear hierarchy is the safest bet. Avoid multiple text columns, tables, or text boxes, which can confuse ATS parsing. Use generous white space and stick to standard section headings (Work Experience, Education, Skills, Patents, etc.) rather than creative alternatives.
1.2 ATS-Compatible Styling
  • Fonts: Use common professional fonts: Calibri, Arial, Times New Roman, or similar.
  • Size: 11–12pt for body text; 14–16pt for section titles.
  • Margins: Standard 1-inch margins improve readability.
  • Consistency: Apply the same format for dates, headings, and bullets so both ATS and recruiters can scan easily.
1.3 Avoid Graphics and Non-Standard Characters
Skip images, graphics, logos, icons, or symbols. Most ATS software cannot interpret them and may reject your file entirely. Avoid emojis and unusual characters. Stick to plain text and standard bullet points.
1.4 Keep Critical Information Out of Headers and Footers
Many ATS ignore header and footer sections entirely. Never put your name, contact information, or anything essential there. Keep it all in the main body of the document.
1.5 File Format Matters
Always submit in the format requested. If unspecified, PDF preserves visual formatting while .docx tends to be the most ATS-friendly. Avoid Pages files or scanned images of resumes.
1.6 Resume Length: Stop Cutting, Start Showing

The "one-page rule" is one of the most harmful pieces of career advice still in circulation. It was designed for an era of printed, manually sorted resumes. That era is over.

Your resume needs to be long enough to demonstrate the depth and impact of your experience, and short enough to stay focused. Cutting meaningful work history to hit an arbitrary page count doesn't make you look disciplined. It makes you look thin.

Early Career · Under 3 Years
1 Page
Appropriate here: you may not have enough material to justify more, and padding it out will hurt you.
Experienced Professionals · 3+ Years
2–3 Pages
Not only acceptable, often necessary. Cramming a meaningful career onto one page forces you to cut the very detail that demonstrates your depth.
The page density rule: Every page in your resume should be at least 75% full. If a page falls below that threshold, you have two options: either add meaningful content to justify it, or remove it entirely and fold that material into the previous page. A half-empty page signals that you ran out of things to say. Don't let formatting undercut your experience.
1.7 Proofread Like Your Career Depends On It
Typos and inconsistent formatting are immediate disqualifiers for many hiring managers. Check grammar, spelling, and ensure dates, fonts, and spacing are consistent throughout. Ask a trusted colleague to review before you send.
1.8 Visual Appeal: For the Human Reading It
ATS compliance and visual clarity aren't mutually exclusive. A well-structured resume with clear section breaks, aligned text, and breathing room signals professionalism before a single word is read. Clean and scannable wins.
02

Keyword Optimization

ATS systems scan for specific words and phrases drawn from the job description. If they're absent from your resume, you may never reach a recruiter's desk, even if you're the most qualified candidate in the pool.

2.1 Tailor Every Application
Generic resumes underperform. A resume customized to the job posting, using that company's language, priorities, and terminology, consistently outperforms a polished but generic version of the same document.
2.2 Match the Job Title
Use the exact job title from the posting in your resume, ideally in your summary or headline. Resumes that echo the exact title are measurably more likely to be flagged as a strong match.
2.3 Pull Keywords Directly From the Posting

Scan each job description for hard skills (tools, software, certifications, equipment) and soft skills (leadership, communication, adaptability). Weave these naturally into your experience bullets and skills section.

Battery Management Systems BMS Electrochemical Testing Scale-Up Process Engineering DFM

Cover both the full term and the abbreviation; ATS systems may search for either.

2.4 Keywords in Context, Not Just in a List
Many ATS rank resumes higher when keywords appear in context (inside your experience bullets or summary), not just stacked in a skills section. Both matter; context matters more.
2.5 Don't Stuff, Distribute
ATS may parse stuffed keywords, but recruiters will notice. Aim to include each important keyword naturally across 2–3 places in the resume. If it reads like a keyword list in disguise, rewrite it.
2.6 Use Tools, But Don't Rely on Them
Resume scanners and JD analyzers can surface gaps quickly. Use them as a checklist, not a ghost-writer. The goal is a resume that reads like a human wrote it and happens to be perfectly optimized.
03

Telling Your Career Story

Content is where resumes are won or lost. Every bullet point is an opportunity to demonstrate value. Most resumes describe what someone did; the best resumes prove what they achieved.

3.1 Lead With Action Verbs
Start every bullet with a strong, specific verb. Words like developed, implemented, scaled, led, reduced, launched, designed, negotiated signal ownership and impact. "Responsible for" and "helped with" do the opposite.
3.2 Achievements Over Duties

The job description already tells hiring managers what the role involves. Your resume needs to answer: what did you accomplish in that role?

✗ Duties-focused Managed social media accounts and created content for multiple platforms.
✓ Achievement-focused Grew LinkedIn following 3× in 8 months through a targeted content strategy, increasing inbound leads by 40%.
3.3 Quantify Everything You Can

Numbers are the fastest way to communicate scale, impact, and credibility. If you can't remember the exact figure, a reasonable estimate is better than nothing.

  • Reduced defect rate from 4.2% to 0.8% across a production line of 12,000 units/month.
  • Generated $1.2M in new revenue in first year, exceeding target by 35%.
  • Cut cycle time by 3 days through process redesign across 4 facilities.
3.4 Concise Bullets, No Padding
Bullets should be 1–2 lines. Recruiters scan quickly: if a line takes more than two reads to understand, it's too long. Clarity and brevity outperform comprehensiveness every time.
3.5 Show Soft Skills: Don't Claim Them

Phrases like "strong communicator" or "team player" are meaningless without evidence. Prove it instead:

✗ Empty claim Strong cross-functional communicator with leadership experience.
✓ Proven through context Led a cross-functional team of 8 across engineering, supply chain, and QA to deliver a product launch 3 weeks ahead of schedule.
3.6 Write a Compelling Summary

A 3–4 sentence summary at the top of your resume sets the frame for everything below. A strong summary covers:

  • Years of experience and domain
  • Your functional specialty or technical expertise
  • Core skills most relevant to the target role
  • One or two standout achievements that signal your caliber
Tip: Your summary is the only section where you can speak in the first person without using "I." Write it as if introducing yourself in a room of your target audience: confident, specific, and direct.
3.7 The 10-Second Test
Recruiters typically spend 7–10 seconds on an initial scan. Ask someone to look at your resume for 10 seconds and then tell you: what role do you apply for, what's your specialty, and what's one impressive thing you've done? If they can't answer all three, reformat or rewrite.
04

A Note for Technical Candidates

Standard resume advice is written for generalist roles. If you're in R&D, engineering, advanced manufacturing, or deep technical specialties, a few additional conventions apply.

4.1 Include Patents and Publications
A dedicated Patents or Publications section is standard and expected in technical roles. List them in a consistent citation format: patent number, title, and filing date. For publications, include journal name and year.
4.2 Certifications and Technical Training
Certifications in specialized areas (Six Sigma, PMP, ISO standards, equipment-specific training) carry real weight in technical hiring. List them with issuing body and date of completion.
4.3 Link Your Portfolio or GitHub
If you have public work (a GitHub repository, research portfolio, project showcase, or conference presentation), include a link. A clean URL in your contact header takes 2 seconds to add and can meaningfully differentiate you.
4.4 Tools, Equipment, and Methodologies
In technical roles, a well-organized skills section listing instruments, software, characterization methods, or manufacturing processes is genuinely useful, not just keyword padding. Organize by category (Analytical Equipment, Simulation Tools, Process Methods, etc.) for readability.
05

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even strong candidates get screened out for preventable reasons. Before submitting, check your resume against this list.

◆
Typos or sloppy formatting
Proofread carefully, twice over. Then ask someone else to read it.
◆
Generic, untailored resume
One-size-fits-all resumes underperform. Customize for every role.
◆
Cutting experience to hit a page count
If you've earned the pages, use them. Thin resumes don't impress anyone.
◆
Keyword stuffing
ATS may read it; recruiters will notice. Keep keywords natural and contextual.
◆
Unexplained gaps
Account for significant gaps with projects, training, caregiving, or volunteering.
◆
Buzzwords without proof
Don't claim it; show it. Replace vague adjectives with concrete outcomes.
◆
Ignoring submission instructions
File type, naming conventions, cover letter requirements: follow them.
◆
Misaligned LinkedIn profile
If your title, dates, or summary don't match your resume, it creates friction.
06

Final Principles

Your resume is a marketing document, not an autobiography. Its one job is to earn you an interview. These principles don't expire.

📄
Treat It as a Living Document
Update with new skills, achievements, and projects regularly: don't wait until you're job searching to remember what you've done.
🎯
Tailor Every Application
Align keywords, skills, and examples to the specific posting. A tailored resume beats a better-written generic one every time.
📊
Lead With Results
Prove your value through measurable outcomes. Numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, and timelines all carry more weight than descriptions.
🔗
Sync Your LinkedIn
Your headline, summary, title history, and dates should align with your resume. Recruiters cross-reference these, and inconsistencies create doubt.
✨
Keep It Polished
One typo or formatting inconsistency can overshadow an otherwise excellent resume. Finish clean: the final 5% of polish matters more than it should.
🤝
Don't Go It Alone
A strong recruiter partner won't just circulate your resume; they'll tell you how to strengthen it for the specific roles and companies you're targeting.
The fundamentals never change: Clarity, relevance, and honesty. The job market will keep evolving: AI screening will get smarter, platforms will come and go, but a resume that clearly communicates who you are, what you've done, and why it matters will always find its audience.
